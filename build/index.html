Cookies and Privacy



Exploring the connectedness of websites through 3rd-party cookies

Cookies are everywhere!

How many cookie disclaimers like "We are using cookies to ..." have you seen today?

... even websites about cookies use cookies!


But...

what is a cookie?

Physical File

A file, stored locally on your computer, that stores your preferences or history for next time you visit


Like a post-it with reminders for tomorrow

Specific

Each website generates it's own cookie to customize your experience, and record data about how you use it


Like a personalized birthday-card (not completely unique... but there is a wide variety)


Cookies Travel

This file is sent to the website when you are browsing to customize it and make you feel at home


Like a pair of slippers, but you carry a different one on your backpack for each place

Private

Only the creator of the cookie can read and modify it


But...




...enter the Cross-Site cookie!

Some websites designate parts of their site to other websites and services that get integrated into the page. These internal mini-websites might be in several websites, and therefore can store information from website A, read it from website B and show you ads based on your behavior on website A



Research

Three researchers from UC Berkeley (Abe Altaweel, Nathan Good and Chris Jay Hoofnagle) performed a "Web Privacy Census" in which they collected web cookies and local storage created by the sites as a web crawler navigated through the top 100 (1000 and 25000) sites.


They analyzed the distribution of several methods of tracking used by these common websites (first-party cookies, third-party cookies, HTML5 local storage, etc...). For more on their research refer to their paper


Here is a selection of some of their results:

  • A total of 6000 cookies and local storage files were created after browsing through the top100 websites
  • Of these 6000 cookies 83% would be third-party cookies
  • 32 websites (out of the 100) placed over 100 cookies

Cookies! Cookies!

Based on this research we wanted to create a visualization that would help show this suprising amount of surveillance-in-cahoots, that may fall unnoticed to standard users.


There have not been many attempts to provide visualizations to this type of data, and the few seem not to be very efficient. An interesting example is provided in here (source) for a similar study performed on mobile apps.

The effort of the project therefore was to create more efficient visualizations for this type of data in order to raise awareness of these facts.


The data was provided by the authors of the Web Privacy Census paper (Abe Altaweel, Nathan Good and Chris Jay Hoofnagle). The analysis was done by our team.


Cross-Site Cookies by Website Type

Given the great complexity of the data obtained, we found it interesting to classify websites according to a set of general categories, to see whether sites would share more cross-site cookies among the same sector, or with different categories.

From the above matrix we can observe most websites have a wide variety cross-site cookies. Let's try to picture it with a more elaborated visualization: a chord diagram.

This graph divides the circle among the different site categories (according to number of sites) and each chord represents the amount of sites from category A that share cookies with category B (if these categories are the same the chord is basically a dome).

Take some time to explore the graph, since it has a lot of information packed into one graph.
